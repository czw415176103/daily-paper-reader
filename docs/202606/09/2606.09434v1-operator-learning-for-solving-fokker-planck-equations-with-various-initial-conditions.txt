LI ZENG † , XIAOLIANG WAN ‡ , YAOBIN WANG § , FABIO NOBILE ¶, AND TAO ZHOU ∥

Abstract. The Fokker-Planck equation (FPE) plays a pivotal role in describing the time evo-lution of probability density functions (PDFs) for systems governed by stochastic dynamics. In this work, we propose a conditional normalizing flow-based physics-informed neural network (PINN) framework for efficiently approximating the solution operator of the FPE for a whole range of initial conditions. Leveraging the Chapman–Kolmogorov equation for Markovian stochastic processes, the problem is reformulated into approximating a transition PDF starting at initial time from a Dirac mass centered at an arbitrary point. The PDF of an associated linearized stochastic differential equation (SDE) is employed as the base distribution for the normalizing flow, providing a good ap-proximation of the target PDF, especially for small times, and thereby avoiding the singularity of the map associated with the Dirac delta initial distribution. Furthermore, a time-weighted loss function is introduced to mitigate numerical instabilities arising at small times, achieving a balance between causality and training difficulty as time progresses. A variety of numerical experiments are presented to illustrate the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method. 

Key words. Conditional Normalizing flow, Fokker-Planck equation, Operator learning

1. Introduction. The FPE describes the time evolution of the PDF associated to a stochastic dynamical system of diffusion type, serving as a deterministic repre-sentation of the stochastic process. However, a variety of FPEs do not admit closed form solutions. Numerically solving the FPE is of high interest in many fields, such as statistical physics, chemistry, biology, finance, and data science, as it enables un-certainty quantification and statistical inference. Classical numerical discretization schemes for this problem include the finite element method [9], the finite difference method [22], the path integral method [43], and the Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto (JKO) scheme [21], among others. Nevertheless, these traditional approaches are compu-tationally challenging since the FPE is often defined over an unbounded physical domain and must satisfy PDF constraints, namely, positivity and integration to one. Grid-based methods (e.g., finite elements/difference) require domain truncation and artificial boundary conditions, and need special care to provide valid PDFs, and scale poorly with the dimension. On the other hand, sampling-based methods (e.g., path integrals and particle-based implementation of JKO) typically require a large number of sampled trajectories, leading to high computational costs. To overcome these challenges, deep learning methods, such as the deep Galerkin method [36], the deep Ritz method [11], and the physics-informed neural networks

In general, deep learning–based methods for solving the FPE can be categorized into two main classes: those that directly repre-sent the solution with neural networks, and those that employ generative modeling techniques. For the first category, the solution of the FPE is parametrized by neural networks with PDF constraints enforced through penalization terms [45, 49, 7, 41, 32]. Alternatively, Bruna et al. [6, 44] approximate the solution of the interacting-particle FPE using a Gaussian mixture model, and numerically solve the ordinary differen-tial equation (ODE) system for the mixture coefficients using the Neural Galerkin method, subsequently normalizing the solution at each time step to enforce the PDF constraints. On the other hand, there is a growing research leveraging generative models to solve FPEs. Normalizing flows have been effectively used to solve the FPE by learning an invertible mapping between a simple base distribution and the target solution of the FPE, which enables direct sampling as well as evaluation of the explicit PDF [38, 13, 47, 48, 29, 12]. Score-based approaches [3, 19, 50] were also developed to solve the FPE, relying on the approximation of the score function, i.e., gradient of the logarithm of the PDF. In parallel, several techniques have been developed for the FPE that exploit the re-interpretation as the L2-Wasserstein gradient flow of the Kullback-Leibler divergence [26, 23]. While a variety of numerical methods have shown promising performance in solv-ing FPEs, there remains a lack of methods capable of efficiently handling multiple initial conditions. This is relevant in many applications, such as ensemble forecasting [24] and data assimilation [30], in which the governing stochastic dynamic is fixed, whereas the initial distribution varies across scenarios or posterior updates. The afore-mentioned methods have to solve the FPE anew every time a new initial condition arrives. This challenge can be viewed from the perspective of operator learning, where the objective is to learn a mapping from an initial distribution to the corresponding time-dependent solution of the FPE. Yang et al. [46] proposed a pseudoreversible normalizing flow and leveraged samples generated from an SDE to learn the terminal solution of the corresponding transition FPE. In the general data-driven setting, gen-erative models are also widely used to learn the conditional PDF, such as conditional Glow [28], monotone generative adversarial networks [2], the gradient of a partially input-convex neural network, and Neural ODE [42], to name a few. For general para-metric PDEs, some techniques have been proposed to learn the solution operator, such as the deep operator network (DeepONet) [27], the Fourier neural operator (FNO) [25], and their variants. Gaby et al. [15] learned the solution operator of the evolution PDEs for various initial conditions by learning a control vector field in the parameter space and solving the ODE system of the neural network parameters. There are also works that learn the Green’s function to construct the solution operator, leveraging the explicit representation of the solution in terms of the Green’s function [1, 5, 39]. However, direct application of these methods to FPEs requires additional treatments of boundary conditions and PDF constraints. In this work, we aim to learn the un-derlying solution operator of the FPE with varying initial conditions. This is achieved as follows. First, we reformulate operator learning for the FPE as the problem of approxi-mating the transition PDF, i.e., the solution of the FPE starting at initial time from a Dirac mass at an arbitrary location x0. This is motivated by the fact that the FPE solution can be expressed as the integral of the product of the transition PDF and the initial distribution, following the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation for Markovian stochastic processes. We develop a conditional normalizing flow parametric in time and location x0 of the Dirac mass, to learn the transition PDF. Benefiting from the OPERATOR LEARNING FOR FOKKER-PLANCK EQUATIONS 3inherent property that a normalizing flow is a model for PDFs, the proposed approach naturally satisfies the PDF constraints without requiring additional enforcement. We mention that the work [31] developed in parallel to ours, also proposes a conditional normalizing flow to approximate the transition PDF, however, in a Neural Galerkin framework. Second, we propose a problem-dependent base distribution to improve the con-ditioning of the conditional normalizing flow. In contrast to the commonly used standard normal distribution, our base distribution, which corresponds to a diffusion process, is given by the explicit solution of a linearized SDE parametric with respect to the initial state x0. The linearized SDE is obtained by applying a first-order Tay-lor expansion to the drift term and a zero-order expansion to the diffusion term of the target SDE. In this way, the conditional normalizing flow defines a transforma-tion mapping the parametric distribution of the target stochastic process to that of a known diffusion process. Using the solution of the linearized SDE as the base random process not only respects the causality for evolution PDEs [8] while bypassing the sin-gular initial condition, but also alleviates training difficulties. We show, in particular, that the total variation distance between the transition PDF of the linearized process and that of the true process is of order O(t) while the Kullback–Leibler divergence scales as O(t2) as t → 0. The corresponding mapping tends to the identity as t → 0, making it easier to learn compared to a more traditional approach that uses a fixed standard normal as a base distribution at all times. Third, we propose a time-weighted loss function to mitigate numerical instabil-ities arising at small times when the transition PDF approaches a Dirac measure. The weight function takes the form td+2 or t d

Introducing this weighting strategy can be interpreted as a trade-off between maintaining causality and alleviat-ing optimization difficulties, as it assigns larger weights to the training points drawn from distributions at later time instances. Fourth, once the transition PDF has been approximated by the conditional nor-malizing flow, we approximate the solution p(x, t ) of the FPE for a given initial distribution p0(x) using an importance-sampling Monte Carlo estimator for the in-tegral of the transition PDF and the initial distribution, the importance sampling measure being a mixture of the initial distribution and an ( x, t )-dependent Gaussian distribution associated to a time reversed linearized SDE. In section 3, we introduce the conditional normalizing flow with base distribution provided by the linearized SDE. In section 4, we analyze the behavior of the loss function as t → 0, and we propose a time-weighted loss and an adaptive sampling algorithm. Importance sampling is discussed in section 5 to efficiently evaluate p(x, t ). Section 6 presents a series of numerical experiments that illustrate the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method, followed by concluding remarks in section 7. 7. Conclusion. In this work, we proposed a conditional normalizing flow-based framework for solving the FPE under various initial conditions. The conditional nor-malizing flow is employed to approximate the transition PDF given any initial state, effectively constructing a mapping from the target stochastic process to a simple base process. The solution of the linearized SDE serves as a suitable candidate for the base stochastic process. We quantified the discrepancy between the PDFs of the linearized and original SDEs, which is characterized by differences in their drift and diffusion terms. Furthermore, we demonstrated that the transformation within the triangu-lar structure connecting the base and target stochastic processes coincides with the Identity mapping at t = 0 and evolves progressively over time. By employing the solution of the linearized SDE and enforcing the identity mapping at t = 0, the initial condition is naturally satisfied by our conditional normalizing flow, thereby bypassing the explicit handling of the Dirac delta initial condition. In addition, we designed a time-weighted loss function to alleviate numerical instabilities, ensuring that the proposed method respects causality in evolution PDEs while accounting for the increasing training difficulty as time progresses. To evaluate p(·, t ) for arbitrary initial conditions over bounded domains, we used an importance sampling strategy to enhance accuracy. A series of numerical experiments validated the effectiveness and robustness. The four-dimensional Beneˇ s SDE test demonstrated that the pro-posed method has the potential for solving moderately high-dimensional FPEs. This research contributes to the numerical solution of complex FPEs by combining the approximation capability of the base model (the linearized SDE) with the expressive power of the conditional normalizing flow. In future work, we will further refine this framework by improving the accuracy of the base stochastic process and exploring its potential applications. Another promising direction is to generalize the proposed method to a broader class of evolutionary partial differential equations. OPERATOR LEARNING FOR FOKKER-PLANCK EQUATIONS 21
