## Multi-Scale Separable Fourier Neural Networks for Solving High-Frequency PDEs 

Qihong Yang a, Qiaolin He a, ∗

> aSchool of Mathematics, Sichuan University, Chengdu, 610065, China

Abstract 

We propose a novel neural network architecture, termed Multi-Scale Sep-arable Fourier Neural Networks (MS-SFNN), for the accurate and efficient solution of linear and nonlinear high-frequency partial differential equations (PDEs). MS-SFNN exploits a separable representation: given a d-dimensional input, it employs d independent subnetworks — each acting on a single co-ordinate — and constructs basis functions via element-wise multiplication of their outputs. The PDE solution is approximated as a linear combination of these basis functions, with coefficients determined by least squares. Crit-ically, all network weights and biases are randomly initialized once, from a uniform distribution with unit variance, and remain fixed thereafter. To en-hance expressivity, a tunable scaling factor is introduced in each subnetwork to modulate the frequency content of the resulting basis functions. Fourier features are explicitly embedded through cosine activations, endowing the method with strong spectral approximation capabilities. To mitigate the memory bottleneck associated with dense collocation in high-frequency or three-dimensional problems, we replace automatic differentiation with an-alytically derived basis function derivatives and develop a memory-efficient batched QR decomposition algorithm for solving large-scale least-squares sys-tems. Numerical experiments demonstrate that MS-SFNN achieves unprece-dented accuracy across a range of challenging PDEs, significantly outper-forming state-of-the-art methods such as Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINN) and Separated-Variable Spectral Neural Networks (SV-SNN).

1. Introduction 

In recent years, neural networks have garnered significant attention in sci-entific computing, particularly following the introduction of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) [1]. PINNs introduced a novel mesh-free frame-work for solving partial differential equations (PDEs), sparking widespread interest and extensive research throughout the computational science com-munity. Unlike traditional numerical methods — such as the finite element method (FEM) [2, 3] or finite difference method (FDM) [4, 5] — which rely on structured or unstructured grids, PINNs approximate the PDE solutions di-rectly using a neural network trained on scattered collocation points. Differ-ential operators are enforced via automatic differentiation, and the network parameters are optimized by minimizing a loss function that incorporates the PDE residual and boundary/initial conditions, typically using optimiz-ers such as Adam [6] or L-BFGS [7]. Although PINNs are capable of solving a wide range of PDEs [8, 9, 10, 11] and have been successfully applied in numerous fields, such as optics [12, 13], fluid mechanics [14], systems biology [15], and biomedicine [16] — they still suffer from limited accuracy compared to traditional methods such as FEM and FDM. To improve the accuracy of PINNs in solving PDEs, researchers have primarily pursued enhancements along four complementary directions. The original PINN formulation [1] employs a simple multiplayer perceptron (MLP) owing to its ease to implementation and universal approximation property. However, numerous recent studies have explored alternative architectures, such as contolutional neural networks (CNNs) [17, 18, 19], recurrent neu-ral networks [20, 21], generative adversarial networks (GANs) [22, 23], Kol-mogorov–Arnold networks (KANs) [24], and Transformers [25, 26]. A sec-ond direction concerns the distribution and adaptivity of training points [8, 27, 28, 29]. It has been argued that uniformly sampled collocation points may inadequately resolve regions exhibiting high gradients or rapid oscil-lations. Adaptive sampling strategies — where points are dynamically re-located or enriched during training based on residual errors or other error indicators — have been shown to significantly improve both convergence and accuracy. A well-known challenge in PINNs is the imbalance loss terms, 2which often exhibit vastly different magnitudes and convergence rates. To address this issue, various adaptive weighting schemes [30, 31, 32] have been proposed to dynamically adjust the relative weight of each loss term dur-ing optimization. Inspired by advances in representation learning, several approaches enhance PINNs by embedding input coordinates [33, 34, 35] into higher-dimensional or frequency-rich spaces. These techniques aim to mitigate spectral bias and enhance the network’s ability to capture high-frequency solution features. Despite these notable advances, none of the proposed modifications has yet enabled PINNs to consistently match the ac-curacy and efficiency of classical numerical methods. Recently, multi-stage training strategies [36, 37] have achieved unprecedented precision by decou-pling the solution process into sequential refinement stages. However, this comes at a significant computational cost: training time increases several-fold compared to standard PINNs, thereby limiting practical ability. Another prominent class of neural-network-based methods for solving PDEs is randomized neural networks (RNNs) [38, 39, 40, 41, 42, 43, 44, 45, 46], which adopt a least-squares formulation and have attracted consid-erable attention owing to their computational efficiency and high accuracy. In this framework, a neural network — typically randomly initialized — is used to construct a set of fixed basis functions. The PDE solution is then approximated as a linear combination of these basis functions, with the unknown coefficients determined by solving a linear least-squares problem rather than via iterative nonlinear optimization. A defining characteristic of RNNs is that all network parameters are frozen after random initializa-tion. This eliminates the need for backpropagation and circumvents the non-convex optimization landscape that plagues standard PINNs, yielding a deterministic, well-conditioned linear system that can be solved robustly and efficiently. The most widely adopted architecture in this paradigm is the single-hidden-layer feedforward neural network (SLFN). Its popularity stems from several advantages: it is simple yet capable of universal approximation, and it introduces sufficient nonlinearity to represent complex solution fea-tures. When combined with automatic differentiation, it incurs significantly lower memory overhead than deep networks. Nevertheless, some researchers argue that the SLFN may lack the sufficient expressive capacity to adequately span the relevant function space. To address this limitation, more sophisti-cated architectures have been developed. A notable example is HLConcELM [47], which enhances representational power through structured hierarchical concatenation. Overall, RNNs offer a compelling alternative to end-to-end 3trained PINNs, particularly when high accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency are prioritized. In this work, we focus on solving high-frequency PDEs using neural net-works. Although neural-network-based methods have achieved remarkable success across various domains, they often struggle significantly when ap-plied to high-frequency PDEs. In particular, standard PINNs frequently fail to capture underlying oscillatory solutions, and even their improved variants [48, 49, 50, 51, 52, 53] — while capable of qualitatively recovering such solu-tions — still fall short of achieving high accuracy. To address this challenge, we propose a novel architecture, termed MS-SFNN (Multi-Scale Separable Fourier Neural Network), specifically designed for high-frequency PDEs. MS-SFNN leverages a separable representation: for a d-dimensional input, it employs d independent single-hidden-layer neural networks, each acting ex-clusively on one coordinate variable. Recognizing that different input dimen-sions may influence the solution at distinct spatial or temporal scales, we introduce a dedicated scaling factor in each subnetwork to modulate the effec-tive frequency content along that dimension. This enables adaptive emphasis on individual variables according to their physical or spectral significance. To further enhance spectral expressivity, we replace conventional activation functions with the cosine function, thereby explicitly embedding Fourier fea-tures into the basis construction. Inspired by the philosophy of RNNs, all internal weights and biases are randomly initialized and subsequently frozen. The final solution is expressed as a linear combination of separable basis functions obtained via element-wise multiplication of the individual subnet-work outputs, and the expansion coefficients are determined by solving a linear least-squares problem. To overcome the severe GPU memory con-straints imposed by the dense collocation points required for high-frequency and three-dimensional problems, we forgo automatic differentiation and in-stead derive and employ analytical expressions for all derivatives of the so-lution. This strategy drastically reduce memory consumption. Additionally, we introduce a batched QR decomposition algorithm that process collocation points in chunks, enabling stable and memory-efficient solution of large-scale least-squares systems. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we briefly review the methodologies of PINNs and RNNs for solving PDEs. Section 3 presents the proposed MS-SFNN architecture together with the batched QR decomposition algorithm designed to alleviate GPU memory constraints. In Section 4, we present a series of numerical experiments on 4high-frequency PDEs to demonstrate the accuracy, efficiency, and robustness of our approach. Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper with a summary of our contributions and a discussion of future research directions.

3.2. Batched QR Decomposition Algorithm 

A major challenge in solving high-frequency problems lies in GPU mem-ory constraints, which arise primarily from two sources: (i) the construction of the system matrix A defined in Equation (6), and (ii) the memory overhead associated with solving the resulting least-squares problem for the coefficient vector w. Since the least-squares solution depends entirely on A, we focus our optimization efforts on its efficient assembly. The memory cost of forming A stems from both its sheer size and the evaluation of the differential operators L and B. To reduce this burden, we replace automatic differentiation with closed-form analytical derivatives — such as those in Equations (13) and (14) — which eliminates computational graphs and significantly lowers memory usage. Nevertheless, when N is very large (e.g., millions of points), even analytical evaluation of LΦ and BΦ can exceed GPU memory capacity. To overcome this limitation, we propose a batched QR decomposition algorithm — inspired by the work of Chen et al. [55] — that incrementally processes collocation points in small batches, ultimately constructing only an M × M upper-triangular matrix. To demonstrate MS-SFNN’s capability in handling nonlinear PDEs, we consider two-dimensional nonlinear elliptic equations. The problem is defined as: (∆u + u2 = f, (x, y ) in Ω,u = g, on ∂Ω, (28) where Ω = (0 , 1) × (0 , 1) , u = ( x + y) cos( kx ) sin( ky ), k = 10 and the corresponding source term f and right term g can be obtained using the exact solution. We begin by constructing the training data using a uniform grid of size 

Nx × Ny = 101 × 101 , which serves as the set of collocation points for both the governing equation the the boundary condition. The number of basis functions is set to M = 10 , 000 . Since this is a two-dimensional problem, MS-SFNN requires two scaling factors, which are chosen as ρ1 = ρ2 = 10 .It should be noted that the underlying PDEs are nonlinear. Therefore, we employ Picard iteration to solve the resulting system. We now provide a detailed description of the Picard iteration procedure employed to solve this nonlinear elliptic equation (28). We begin by assuming an initial guess w0, which is randomly generated using the Xavier initializa-tion method [62]. This yields an initial approximate solution u0(x, y ) = 

Φ(x, y ) · w0, where Φ(x, y ) denotes the vector of MS-SFNN basis functions evaluated at (x, y ). At each Picard iteration i, for i ≥ 1, the original nonlin-ear PDEs are linearized around the current approximation ui. Specifically, the nonlinear term is frozen using the previous iterate, resulting in the lin-earized governing equation ∆u + uiu = f . The linearized boundary-value problem is then solved using the MS-SFNN framework combined with a least squares method. This yields an update coefficient vector wi+1 and the cor-responding approximate solution ui+1 (x, y ) = Φ(x, y ) · wi+1 . The iterations proceeds until convergence is achieved. In our implementation, we set a maximum of 100 iterations. Convergence (or early termination) is triggered when the absolute difference between successive coefficient vectors satisfies 

∥wi+1 − wi∥ ≤ 10 −16 . The final converged coefficient vector w∗ defines the desired high-accuracy approximate solution u∗(x, y ) = Φ(x, y ) · w∗ to the original nonlinear problem. Although PINN is able to produce a solution, its accuracy is 30 limited to an error level of approimxately 10 −1. SV-SNN improves upon this, achieving errors on the order of 10 −3. In stark contrast, MS-SFNN attains dramatically higher precision, with both L∞ and L2 errors reaching the or-der of 10 −13 . This clearly demonstrates that MS-SFNN is capable of solving this nonlinear elliptic equation with exceptional accuracy. In this work, we propose Multi-Scale Separable Fourier Neural Networks (MS-SFNN) for solving both linear and nonlinear high-frequency PDEs. The core idea of MS-SFNN is based on a separable representation. For a d-dimensional input, we employ d independent subnetworks, each processing one coordinate direction. The outputs of these subnetworks are then mul-tiplied element-wise to construct a set of separable basis functions. The solution to the PDEs is approximated as a linear combination of these basis functions, where the coefficients are determined by the least squares method. Importantly, the weights and biases in each subnetwork are initialized once using a uniform distribution with variance 1 and kept fixed thereafter. How-ever, such a fixed initialization alone provides limited representational capac-ity and may be insufficient for accurately resolving solutions across diverse scenarios, especially those involving high-frequency features. To overcome this limitation, we introduce a scaling factor in front of the fixed weights and biases within each subnetwork. This scaling factor effectively controls the frequency content of the resulting basis functions and significantly enhances the expressive power of the network. Since each subnetwork has its own scaling factor, the overall MS-SFNN architecture contains multiple scaling factors. Finally, to further enrich the spectral representation, we explicitly incorporate Fourier features by using the cosine function as the activation in the construction of the basis functions. This design endows MS-SFNN with strong capabilities in capturing oscillatory and high-frequency behav-ior,making it particularly well-suited for challenging high-frequency PDEs. When dealing with high-frequency PDEs or extending the problem from two to three dimensions, a large number of collocation points are required to accurately resolve the fine-scale oscillatory features. This dramatically increases memory demand and often leads to GPU memory exhaustion due to the storage and computation overhead of the full basis matrix and its derivatives. To address this challenge, we propose two key algorithmic in-novations. In stead of relying on automatic differentiation, which incurs significant memory and computational costs during backpropagation, we de-rive and use closed-form analytical expressions for all required spatial and temporal derivatives of basis function obtained from MS-SFNN. This elim-inates the need to store computational graphs and drastically reduce both memory footprint and evaluation time. To solve the resulting large-scale least-squares system without loading the entire basis matrix into GPU mem-42 ory, we develop a memory-efficient, batched QR decomposition algorithm. This approach process the collocation points in smaller chunks, incrementally building an orthogonal representation of the basis space while maintaining numerical stability. Together, these strategies significantly alleviate GPU memory pressure and enable MS-SFNN to scale effectively to high-frequency regimes and three-dimensional problems. As a result, MS-SFNN remains computationally feasible and highly accurate even in these demanding set-tings. Although the proposed MS-SFNN demonstrate outstanding performance in numerical experiments, several important limitations remain and point to promising directions to future research. The accuracy of MS-SFNN is highly dependent on the choice of scaling factors. Poorly selected values can lead to significant degradation in solution quality, sometimes by many orders of magnitude. While a brute-force grid search can be used to identify suit-able scaling factors, this approach is computational expensive and impracti-cal for high-dimensional and multi-variable problems. Therefore, developing an efficient and robust algorithm for automatically discovering near-optimal scaling factors is a critical next step. Furthermore, numerical results in-dicate that when the problem involves very high frequencies or extends to three dimensional high-frequency settings, the method exhibits a noticeable loss of precision. This suggests that the current basis construction may not fully resolve the most challenging spectral content. Future work should focus on designing enhanced basis architectures that can maintain high accuracy across ultra-high-frequency and three-dimensional scenarios.
