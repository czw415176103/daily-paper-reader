# Structure Over Nonlinearity: Explicit Interaction Architectures for Dynamical Learning 

Augusto Sarti ∗

June 18, 2026 

Abstract 

Most learning architectures for dynamical systems rely on generic nonlinear function ap-proximation, often requiring high model complexity to capture structured behaviors. In this work, we propose an alternative paradigm in which modeling capability arises primarily from structure rather than from expressive nonlinearities. We introduce a class of explicit structured dynamical units based on wave-inspired interaction structures with internal state. Inspired by wave-based computational principles, the proposed units adopt a strictly causal organization that eliminates algebraic loops, yielding fully explicit models that can be evaluated without implicit solvers. Stacking such units produces layered dynamical architectures with emergent hierarchical be-havior. Through experiments on a nonlinear system identification task, we show that depth improves both representation quality and generalization, even under limited parameter opti-mization. In particular, the proposed architectures produce informative internal representations even under readout-only fitting, indicating that useful dynamical structure emerges from the organization of interactions prior to substantial parameter optimization. These results suggest that structure-first design provides a viable and effective alternative to conventional black-box approaches for learning dynamical systems, highlighting the role of interaction structure as a primary source of model expressivity. 

# 1 Introduction 

Learning dynamical systems from data is a central problem in signal processing, control, and machine learning. Modern approaches typically rely on highly expressive nonlinear function approximators, such as recurrent neural networks [1, 2] or modern deep learning architectures [3], which aim to capture complex temporal behaviors through generic nonlinear mappings. While such models are flexible, they often require large numbers of parameters and extensive training, and provide limited insight into the underlying system structure. More importantly, their expressive power is largely derived from nonlinear function approximation, rather than from the organization of the model itself. As a result, they may struggle to efficiently capture structured dy-namical phenomena, particularly when data are limited or when interpretability is desired. Recent approaches have attempted to introduce stronger inductive bias through physics-inspired formula-tions, including neural differential equations and physics-informed learning [4–6].

In this work, we explore an alternative paradigm in which modeling capability arises primarily from structure , rather than from generic nonlinearities. The key idea is to design computational units whose internal organization encodes meaningful dynamical interactions, thereby providing a strong inductive bias without relying on highly expressive nonlinear components. This perspective is related to broader efforts in structured learning and inductive bias design [7]. We introduce a class of structured dynamical units based on explicit wave-inspired interaction structures with internal state, inspired by wave digital structures [8]. Unlike classical wave digital formulations, which typically lead to implicit fixed-point relations due to algebraic coupling, the proposed units adopt a strictly causal, layered organization that eliminates algebraic loops. This yields a fully explicit computational structure, where all updates can be scheduled sequentially without requiring implicit solvers. This distinction is critical for scalable learning architectures, since implicit interaction models generally require iterative fixed-point procedures whose computational complexity and convergence properties become increasingly problematic in deep or adaptive settings. In this sense, the proposed units can be interpreted as an explicit realization of wave-based struc-tured computation, trading algebraic closure for causal organization, while preserving the essential interaction principles underlying wave-based models. A central question is whether these units can be composed into deeper architectures in a mean-ingful way. Unlike conventional neural networks, where depth primarily increases representational capacity, stacking structured dynamical units has the potential to induce hierarchical dynamical behavior across layers. To investigate this, we construct layered models by stacking multiple units and evaluate their performance on a nonlinear system identification task. We consider controlled training scenarios in which only a subset of parameters is optimized, allowing us to isolate the effect of structure and depth. Our results show that: 

• Structured dynamical units produce meaningful representations even with minimal training, as evidenced by readout-only fitting. 

• Stacking such units improves generalization performance on unseen data. 

• The observed improvements arise from the internal organization of the model, rather than from increased nonlinear expressivity. These findings support the view that structure-first design can provide an effective alternative to conventional black-box approaches for learning dynamical systems. Rather than relying on increasingly expressive nonlinear models, it may be possible to achieve strong performance through carefully designed dynamical architectures with built-in inductive bias. The proposed framework demonstrates that explicit structured units can be composed into layered architectures with improved generalization properties, suggesting that carefully organized interaction structures can serve as a primary source of model expressivity in dynamical learning systems. This perspective is also consistent with classical system-theoretic views, where structure and interconnection play a primary role in shaping dynamical behavior. This work should be interpreted as a first learnable instantiation of a broader class of structured computational architectures based on wave digital principles, which we refer to as Wave Digital 2Computational Structures (WDCS). In this perspective, the proposed model is not merely a recur-rent architecture, but a particular explicit realization of a structured computational paradigm in which dynamics arise from the composition of localized interactions. The present paper focuses on learnability within this explicit setting. The main contributions of this work are: 

• A structured dynamical unit based on wave-inspired interaction structures with internal state. 

• A layered architecture constructed from such units, enabling hierarchical dynamical modeling. 

• Experimental evidence that structure and depth improve generalization in nonlinear system identification tasks. A key distinction of the proposed approach is that structure is not induced through parametriza-tion, but explicitly encoded in the interaction pattern of the computational units. In contrast to conventional recurrent or neural differential models, where dynamical behavior emerges from learned nonlinear mappings, the proposed architecture constrains admissible behaviors through its organi-zation. This leads to a form of structured computation in which expressivity is governed by the composition of local interactions, rather than by the capacity of generic function approximators, providing an intrinsic inductive bias that is independent of training.

# 4 Relation to Structured Recurrent and Reservoir Models 

The proposed framework is related to recurrent and reservoir-based approaches in that it uses internal dynamical states to generate representations of input histories. However, the source of representation is different. In reservoir computing, useful features often emerge from a fixed or randomly initialized recurrent system followed by a trained readout. In the present framework, the internal organization is not random or generic, but explicitly structured through localized interaction patterns inspired by wave-based computation. This distinction is central to the structure-first viewpoint developed in this paper. The goal is not merely to exploit a high-dimensional recurrent state, but to organize the computation so that useful dynamical coordinates arise from structured interactions among local transformations and internal state variables. The readout-only experiments are therefore interpreted not only as a reduction in training complexity, but as evidence that the interaction structure itself induces informative internal representations. 

# 5 Explicit Computability 

A central property of the proposed units is that all computations are explicitly scheduled. At each time index n, the variables z1, z2, bn, and ξn+1 are computed sequentially from the known quantities 

an and ξn. No variable depends instantaneously on itself or on another quantity that must be solved simultaneously. 

Proposition: The structured dynamical unit defined by Eqs. (1)–(4) is explicitly computable at each time step without iterative fixed-point solution. 

Proof: Given an and ξn, the intermediate variable z1 is computed directly from (1). Then z2 is computed from (2), using only already available quantities. The output bn is then computed from (3), and the next state ξn+1 from (4). Therefore, the update can be evaluated by a finite sequence of explicit operations, with no algebraic loop.

# 6 Experimental Setup 

Task – We consider a nonlinear system identification problem, in which the goal is to predict the output of a dynamical system from an input sequence. The input signal combines smooth stochastic components, impulsive events, and slowly varying oscillatory behavior, in order to excite multiple dynamical regimes. 

Models – We compare two structured dynamical models: 

• A one-layer model, consisting of a single structured dynamical unit 

• A two-layer model, obtained by stacking two units as described in Section 3 Both models share the same internal structure and differ only in their depth. 

Training Protocol – We evaluate three configurations of increasing complexity: 

• Fixed parameters (no training) 

• Readout-only least-squares fitting 

• Partial optimization of the final stage parameters Optimization is performed using a derivative-free procedure, and a train/validation split is used to assess generalization performance on unseen data. The input signal is designed to excite different dynamical regimes, including smooth variations, impulsive events, and oscillatory components. This ensures that the identification task requires the model to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies. The train/validation split is chosen to prevent temporal leakage, and performance is evaluated exclusively on unseen data. This setup allows us to isolate the effect of model structure and depth on generalization. 67 Results

# 8 Discussion 

The results of this study highlight a qualitative difference between conventional nonlinear models and the structured dynamical approach proposed here. While both one-layer and two-layer models share the same basic computational building blocks, stacking structured units leads to consistent improvements in generalization performance. A key observation is that these improvements are already visible under highly constrained train-ing conditions. In particular, the readout-only experiments show that the internal representations produced by the structured units are informative even before any adaptation of internal parameters. This suggests that the performance gains are not primarily due to increased parameter flexibility, but rather to the inductive bias introduced by the model structure. This behavior suggests that the proposed architectures possess an intrinsic organizational induc-tive bias, in which useful dynamical coordinates emerge from the interaction structure itself rather than from extensive parameter adaptation. From this perspective, depth plays a role that differs from its interpretation in standard deep learning architectures. Instead of merely increasing the capacity of a function approximator, stacking structured dynamical units appears to induce a form of hierarchical dynamical processing. The first layer captures local transformations driven by the input, while subsequent layers refine and integrate these representations, leading to improved predictive performance. The proposed units are fully explicit and computable through scheduled updates, avoiding the implicit solves required by many physically inspired models and enabling scalable layered architec-tures compatible with learning frameworks. The present study is intentionally limited in scope: experiments focus on a controlled nonlinear system identification task with partial parameter optimization, and no explicit physical constraints (e.g., passivity or energy conservation) are enforced. This suggests that the internal representation induced by the proposed structure captures relevant dynamical features independently of parameter optimization. Future work will address several open directions. First, a more complete theoretical character-ization of the proposed units, including their relation to wave digital structures and graph-based formulations, will be developed. Second, training strategies that exploit the structure of the model 8more fully will be investigated. Finally, the incorporation of physically motivated constraints may enable the construction of deep architectures that combine learnability with strong guarantees on stability and interpretability. 

Interpretation as Structured Dynamical Modeling 

The proposed approach can be interpreted as an instance of structured dynamical modeling, in which the hypothesis space is constrained by the internal organization of the computational units rather than by the choice of a generic function class. In conventional learning architectures, model expressivity is typically achieved by increasing the complexity of nonlinear mappings. In contrast, the present framework relies on fixed interaction patterns and state evolution mechanisms that encode prior assumptions about the system structure. From this viewpoint, learning is not primarily concerned with discovering arbitrary input-output mappings, but with adapting a structured dynamical system whose behavior is already shaped by its internal organization. This leads to a reduced effective hypothesis space, which may explain the improved generalization observed in the experiments. Moreover, the explicit and modular nature of the proposed units suggests a connection with physically grounded modeling approaches [4–6]. While no explicit physical constraints are enforced in this work, the structure of the units is compatible with extensions that incorporate properties such as stability, passivity, or energy conservation. This perspective opens the possibility of bridging data-driven learning and physically motivated modeling, by constructing architectures that are both learnable and structurally constrained. 

# 9 Conclusion 

We introduced a class of explicit structured dynamical units and showed that their composition into layered architectures improves generalization in a nonlinear system identification task. The experiments indicate that meaningful internal representations can emerge from the orga-nization of local interactions even under highly constrained training conditions. In particular, the observed improvements obtained through layered composition suggest that structure itself may serve as a primary source of model expressivity in dynamical learning systems. More broadly, the proposed framework suggests an alternative perspective on learning dynam-ical systems, in which computational capability arises not primarily from increasingly expressive nonlinear mappings, but from the organization and composition of structured dynamical interac-tions. These results should be viewed as preliminary evidence that structured interaction architectures provide a viable route toward learnable dynamical systems with built-in inductive bias. The key mes-sage is not that the proposed architecture outperforms generic recurrent models on standard bench-marks — a claim that would require more extensive experimentation — but rather that interaction structure itself constitutes a primary source of dynamical expressivity, independently of parameter optimization. This reframing of the role of architecture in dynamical learning motivates a broader research programme on structure-aware computational systems, connecting operator-theoretic foun-dations for nonlinear systems with memory, wave-based computational structures, and data-driven learning within a unified framework. The present work represents one explicit instantiation of this programme; companion contributions, currently in preparation, develop the underlying opera-9tor theory, the formal Wave Digital Computational Structure framework, and passivity-preserving learnable cells with Lyapunov stability guarantees.
