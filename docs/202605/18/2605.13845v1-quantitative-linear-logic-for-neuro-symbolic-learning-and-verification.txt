## Abstract 

Differentiable Logics are deployed in neuro-symbolic learning tasks as a way of embedding logical constraints in the training objective of neural networks. A differentiable logic consists of a syntax to write logical properties and a semantics to interpret them as real-valued functions to be folded in the loss function. A defining trade-off of the field is that between logical properties of the connectives, and analytic concerns for the semantics, with both aspects being relevant in applications. At one extreme we find fuzzy logics, that have well-established algebraic and proof-theoretic foundations, and at the other ad-hoc differentiable logics like Fischer’s DL2, conceived for deep learning applications. However, no satisfactory foundation has emerged yet. We propose a resolution to this long-standing tension via a novel logic, Quantitative Linear Logic ( QLL ), with foundational ambitions. Our design is driven by naturality —the idea that, since logical constraints are translated to losses, the semantics of the connectives should be pertinent operations used in ML practice (that is, sum and log-sum-exp) on additive quantities (like logits). We then judge the result on two aspects: logical adequacy —that they satisfy most of the standard logical laws of Linear Logic; and empirical effectiveness —test-time performance (as measured by adversarial attacks) is well-correlated to the actual verification of the logical constraints (as measured by off-the-shelf neural network verifiers), which makes QLL stand out among SoTA techniques. 

## 1 Introduction 

Neuro-symbolic machine learning (NeSy ML) is a term that refers to methods that combine data-driven optimisation (“the neural") with logical reasoning (“the symbolic"). The slogan ‘The future is neuro-symbolic’ [Belle and Marcus, 2026] is evidenced by a range of notable successes, from the flagship project on Alpha Geometry [Trinh et al., 2024] to numerous compendia of practical neuro-symbolic methods [Sarker and Eberhart, 2023]. Neuro-symbolic tasks occur in every area of machine learning. In cyber-physical systems, for example, models such as the famous ACAS Xu [Katz et al., 2017, 2019] are obtained by training a neural aircraft controller to avoid mid-air collision in a way that is optimised relative to the prior data, but at the same time, ensuring that certain unsafe decisions never occur by mistake. These unsafe scenarios exist as prior knowledge of the engineers, and can be formally specified and verified as logical constraints that restrict the neural network output for certain regions of the input space. In computer vision, logical constraints arise when classes of images naturally organize hierarchically.

The requirement for classification to remain the same for all data within small ε-balls of the data set samples is a requirement that is, in essence, extraneous to the question of optimising the model for the data as given.

Then these three major categories of the NeSy ML tasks (reflecting prior knowledge of the physical world, encoding class relations, and robustness) can be mixed in a variety of ways. For example, consider a computer vis-ion task in which we train a multi-label classifier 

f : R3×28 ×28 ! R6 to recognise the pips on the visible faces of a die from a 28 × 28 RGB im-age. We have some prior knowledge that some combinations of visible faces are physically im-possible. We can form superclasses of permiss-ible combinations (e.g. (2 , 4, 6) , (4 , 2, 6) , (6 , 2, 4) , etc. form one superclass), and we can specify a logical constraint that says that within ε-vicinity of each image in the data set, the classification will only fall within a permissible superclass. Thus, in addition to using visual data (see Fig. 1), we want to incorporate prior knowledge about relationships between visible faces during training, i.e. we want to teach the classifier to predict only physically possible combinations of pips. It has been noticed long ago (most famously, but not exclusively, in [Szegedy et al., 2014]) that models trained only on data fail to satisfy logical constraints. This gave rise to different flavors of 

property-driven training [Giunchiglia et al., 2022]. The goal of this group of methods is to establish a balance between prediction performance and satisfaction of the chosen logical constraint, irrespective of whether it is entailed by the data or not. Eventually, we may want to guarantee (i.e. formally verify) that the model adheres to the property (see Related work below). Some safety-critical systems with neural network components are modelled, verified, and trained in that way. 

Differentiable logic (DL) is one specific methodology for property-driven training. Unlike other methods surveyed e.g. by Giunchiglia et al. [2022], it does not make any assumption about the architecture of the model or the kind of data, and can be used to augment any optimisation task. Given a property ϕ(θ) that concerns an ML model fθ with trainable parameters θ, the goal is to define a loss function JϕK(θ) that can be optimised to find θ∗ satisfying ϕ. In principle, ϕ can be an arbitrary propositional formula that includes logical connectives and (∧), or (∨), negation (¬), etc.—a DL then interprets such connectives as operations over suitable subsets of the reals. 

Deep Learning with Differentiable Logics (DL2 ) [Fischer et al., 2019] was one of the first differen-tiable logics proposed specifically for ML. ‘Robustness metrics’ for Signal Temporal Logic ( STL )(a temporal extension of classical propositional logic) were proposed by Varnai and Dimarogonas [2020], with connectives adapted specifically to the gradient-based optimisation, and further invest-igated in [Chevallier et al., 2025]. Fuzzy logics are also natural candidates, given their established logical tradition and well-understood proof theory—indeed the usage of well-studied t-norms , which give semantics to fuzzy logic connectives, was investigated by van Krieken et al. [2022].

Solution: QLL . We propose a resolution to this state of affairs in a logic we call Quantitative Linear Logic (QLL ). It combines many lessons learned from other differentiable logics, and organizes them according to a principle we call naturality . That means, its semantics uses operations which are already established in statistics, probability theory, or machine learning. Naturality stems from the observation that even dimensionless numbers are implicitly typed by the way they are used, and our choice of semantics must be type-correct in this sense. Intuitively, logits, information, entropy, energy, and distances are additive quantities, as independent such quantities combine by sum. Most commonly prediction loss functions fall in this category (usually because they are either distances or entropies), thus our semantics must land in [−∞ , +∞]+ = ([ −∞ , +∞], ≥

, 0, +) (note the reversed order). Related Work. Techniques for property-driven learning range from incorporating constraints into the training objective to enforcing them directly in the model architecture. Early approaches include Semantic-based Regularisation ( SBR ) [Diligenti et al., 2017], Logic Tensor Networks [Serafini and d’Avila Garcez, 2016], and probabilistic frameworks such as DeepProbLog [Manhaeve et al., 2018] and Semantic Loss [Xu et al., 2018]. More recent efforts have focused on integrating logical constraints into a dedicated neural network layer, ensuring constraint satisfaction by design, e.g., 4CCN+ [Giunchiglia et al., 2024] and Semantic Probabilistic Layer ( SPL ) [Ahmed et al., 2022], while approaches such as Disjunctive Refinement Layer ( DRL ) [Stoian and Giunchiglia, 2024] and Probabilistic Algebraic Layer ( PAL ) [Kurscheidt et al., 2025] extend this idea to non-convex constraints. Our work addresses the methods that develop special purpose loss functions, and as such, our loss functions can complement any architectures, including the neuro-symbolic architectures mentioned above.

On the side of formal verification of neural net-works, a wide range of tools is available. They mainly differ by the algorithms they deploy: lin-ear programming and Satisfiability Modulo The-ories ( SMT )-solving underlie Marabou [Katz et al., 2019, Wu et al., 2024], reachability-based methods underlie NNV [Tran et al., 2020, Lopez et al., 2023] and PyRAT [Lemesle et al., 2025, 2026], and linear bound propagation techniques gave rise to α, β -CROWN [Zhang et al., 2018, Xu et al., 2020, 2021, Wang et al., 2021] (see also [Huang et al., 2020, Urban and Miné, 2021, Liu et al., 2021, Albarghouthi, 2021]). In this work, we rely on Marabou, as it admits more general class of properties that some other solv-ers. We use Marabou through Vehicle [Daggitt et al., 2025], a domain-specific language that provides a user-friendly interface for writing complex logical properties. Given a logical specification for an ML model, one can either formally verify that it holds, or, failing that, retrain the model to satisfy it; and then make a verification attempt again. This set up of using a single specification for both formal verification and training is commonly referred to as continuous verification [Daggitt et al., 2025, Casadio et al., 2022] and is depicted in Fig. 2. The evaluation we present in Section 4 follows this pattern: training and verification are done with the same formula ϕ.

## 3 Quantitative Linear Logic 

We work in the extended reals [−∞ , +∞], equipped with the reverse order: −∞ is maximally true and +∞ is maximally false. This convention matches the loss-function intuition that lower numbers are more true . Indeed, losses are usually subject to minimization (think energy, entropy, distance). As noted in the introduction, [−∞ , +∞] is the natural domain of addition. This will be our ‘primary’ connective in this setting: all the other ones are obtained from this one following the template of Linear Logic 3 [Girard, 1995]. The first, ∪p is disjunctive while the second is conjunctive ∩p. ## 4 Evaluation of QLL 

In this section we evaluate QLL against other differentiable logics based on their ability to jointly achieve high prediction performance and satisfy a given logical constraint on a classification task. Further details on the experimental setup, datasets, model architectures, and evaluation methodology are provided in Appendix A.5. The experiments were run using a framework for property-driven training we implemented in PyTorch [Paszke et al., 2019] with the goal to provide a reusable, general purpose framework for training neural networks with logical constraints using various differentiable logics. The framework, including all code, data, and scripts required to reproduce the results presented in this paper, is available at https://github.com/tflinkow/property-driven-ml .

4.1 Experimental Set Up: Exhaustive Verification vs Testing and Adversarial Attacks 

Given a logical constraint ϕ, there are three ways in which adherence of a model to the constraint can be measured. We list them in the order of increasing rigor. The standard mathematical definitions of Constraint Security (CSec) and Verified Constraint Satisfaction (CSat), following [Casadio et al., 2022], are given in Definitions A.1 to A.3 in Appendix A.6. • Constraint Security ( CSec ) evaluates whether a PGD attack [Madry et al., 2018] can discover counterexamples to ϕ in Bε( ˆ x). This is the de facto standard ML method of finding examples violating ϕ, see [Fischer et al., 2019]. Effectiveness of this search varies based on the loss, and it comes with no formal guarantee of finding a counterexample if one exists 7.• Verified Constraint Satisfaction ( CSat ) evaluates whether ϕ holds for all possible samples in Bε( ˆ x).This measurement, also called formal verification , is the most rigorous, and gives sound and complete guarantees of finding a counterexample if one exists. We adhere to standard methods of formal verification with Marabou [Katz et al., 2019, Wu et al., 2024] and Vehicle [Daggitt et al., 2025]. All metrics are evaluated on the test sets. Standard prediction accuracy of models is denoted Prediction Accuracy ( PAcc ). The CSat metric, which exhaustively analyses the entire Bε( ˆ x) for a data point ˆx,is obtained from a fixed randomly chosen subset of the test set. For all experiments, we report the mean and standard deviation (in % ) across 5 random seeds for all metrics. 

Given a property ϕ, CSec , seen as robustness to attack on ϕ, is thus always an optimistic approxima-tion of CSat for ϕ. Our experiments in Tables 5 and 13 show that typically, this over-approximation is vastly unrepresentative. Additionally, the gap between CSec and CSat is consistently lower when we train with QLL .

4.2 Performance across different kinds of NeSy tasks 

Our evaluation covers three representative categories of neuro-symbolic learning tasks introduced in Section 1. 4.3 Effect of the Interval Choice 

Prior differentiable logic design made different choices for the interval on which loss functions are defined: fuzzy differentiable logics [van Krieken et al., 2022] work on the interval [0 , 1] , DL2 [Fischer et al., 2019]—on [0 , ∞), and STL [Varnai and Dimarogonas, 2020]—on (−∞ , ∞). Recall that in QLL, the choice of the interval [−∞ , +∞] is motivated by naturality, specifically that it is the range for cross-entropy, which is used as prediction loss. Thus, the choice of the interval and the choice of operations are—via the naturality principle—the two sides of one coin. To test the effect of the interval choice alone, it suffices to compare the performance of STL/QLL vs DL2 on the strong classification robustness property that does not involve any conjunctions or disjunctions (all three logics interpret comparisons in the same way).

4.4 Effect of Convergence and Softness Parameters 

The next property of QLL that we hold valuable is the fact that non-linear connectives in both logics converge to min and max as p ! ∞. STL shares the same feature, whereby its connectives are parameterised by a parameter ν with the same limit as ν ! ∞. Our hypothesis is that this property conveys better the effect of classical connectives at training time. 

Tables 5 and 13 show that having additive approximations of min /max is useful: on three out of four properties, STL outperforms most other logics and QLL outperforms all other logics. We note, however, that current experiments are inconclusive when we try to isolate the effect of the interval choice from the effect of min and max approximations, where the former might overshadow the latter. This can be seen on the fuzzy logics, where Gödel directly uses min and max , Yager smoothly approximates min and max , and product uses neither. Still, all three fuzzy logics perform similarly. 

4.5 Gradients and Effect of Shadow-Lifting

## 5 Limitations and future work 

From a theoretical standpoint, we left open various issues. The biggest limitation is being not able to adequately explain for the empirical behaviour of QLL : intuitively, QLL better manages the gap between the soft spec JϕK and the hard one JϕKB because the soft connectives approximate the hard ones and due to logical adequacy. However, we do not prove rigorous bounds linking the test-time logical constraint loss value to the satisfaction of the hard spec. Doing so would be enticing since verification is a very expensive process, compared to training, thus for complex models, having guarantees on verification by training only would be of great practical value. The reason for this failure is that JϕK appears buried within a complex expression we cannot fully make sense of logically yet. The ideas of Capucci [2024], Flinkow et al. [2025] suggest this requires a first-order extension of QLL . DL2 is superior in this sense, having a first-order language, but not a proper first-order logic (with proof rules, model theory, etc.) From an empirical standpoint, our experiments are purely demonstrative and cannot be considered to reflect real-world applications, based on model and dataset size as well as complexity of the logical constraint.

A.1 Residuated Lattices 

Section 3 introduced the semantics of QLL in [−∞ , +∞] as exhibiting a ‘quantitative’ version of a 

residuated lattice . This appendix unpacks what that means. We reference Galatos et al. [2007] and forthcoming work on the proof and model theory of QLL [Capucci et al., 2026]. Residuated lattices are the standard algebraic semantics of substructural logics (like linear and fuzzy logic), and the laws collected in Section 3 are quantitative instances of laws that hold in any residuated lattice. 14 Lattices. A partially ordered set , or poset , is a pair (L, ≤) where ≤ is reflexive ( a ≤ a), transitive (a ≤ b and b ≤ c implies a ≤ c), and antisymmetric ( a ≤ b implies b ≰ a). Given two elements 

a, b ∈ L, their join a ∨ b is their least upper bound and their meet a ∧ b is their greatest lower bound, when these exist. The ‘least upper bound’ property of the join a ∨ b is characterized by two clauses: 

c ≤ a or c ≤ b then c ≤ a ∨ b, (upper bound) 

a ≤ c and b ≤ c then a ∨ b ≤ c. (minimality) The first says a ∨ b dominates each of a and b; the second says it is the smallest such element. The meet a ∧ b is characterized dually as a greatest lower bound. Then, a lattice is a poset in which every pair of elements admits both a join and a meet. Two examples of lattices relevant to this work are the extended reals ([ −∞ , +∞], ≥), so that 

a ∨ b = min( a, b ) and a ∧ b = max( a, b ); and the Booleans B = {false , true } with false ≤ true ,where ∨ is logical disjunction and ∧ is logical conjunction. The main idea behind the algebraic semantics of QLL is to replace the order ≤ with a quantitative predicate instead, which is ⊸ in [−∞ , +∞]. This makes it a kind of ‘asymmetric’ metric space, as observed already by Lawvere [1973]. Thus (upper bound) and (minimality) become the clauses appearing in Section 3 as the disjunction rules of QLL . The same happens for the conjunction rules ,which are in fact formal duals.

In each experiment, all runs were trained under identical fixed hyperparameters, i.e., learning rate, adversarial attack configurations, and loss balancing. A.6 Evaluation Metrics

The Dice dataset is a small custom multi-label dataset consisting of 340 RGB images of size 

28 × 28 . Each image shows three visible faces of a die. Labels are multi-hot vectors in {0, 1}6,where each ground-truth label contains exactly three positive entries. The dataset was created by randomly rolling and photographing physical dice and manually annotating the visible faces in each image. For MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, we use a small convolutional network with two strided 3 × 3

convolutional layers with 4 and 8 channels, respectively. The first convolutional layer is followed by a ReLU activation. The resulting representation is flattened and passed through a fully-connected layer with a ReLU activation, followed by a final linear layer mapping to 10 logits. The Dice dataset uses a similar architecture, except that the first convolutional layer takes RGB images as inputs, and the final linear layer maps to 6 logits.
